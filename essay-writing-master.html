At A-level you have to gesture at arguments. At degree-level you have to argue.
At A-level you have to know, at degree-level you have to show.
Knowing
  • Hit the markscheme
  • Name the arguments
  • Make the right claim
Showing
  • Give an account
  • Argue
  • Defend a case

When you write this essay, you have a period of time measured in minutes to think of the answer, write it down, check it and move on to the next. When you write a formative or summative essay, by contrast, you have a period of time measured in weeks or months to reflect on your subject, read up on it, argue about it, and finally commit to paper a considered discussion of your claims in the light of the texts on which they stand. And we have no markscheme, only a set of grade descriptions (which are in the undergrad handbook) and our own instinct and knowledge of the subject to guide us.
We have no markscheme.

Anselm's account of God in the Ontological Argument

Anselm says that God is the greatest conceivable being.
  • Where? The proslogion has two “ontological arguments”.
    • Ch. 2: That truly there is a God.
    • Ch. 3: That He cannot be thought not to exist
  • → reading of Chs. 2 & 3
    • Than which nothing greater can be thought
    • Than which nothing better can be thought
Than which nothing greater/better can be thought
  • What does it mean to think of the “nothing greater”?
  • Sokolowski: “context of disclosure”
    • What is a context of disclosure?
  • → reading of Sokolowski
    • Kind of attention paid to an object
    • “background” against which “foreground” is picked out.
    • → edge of thought; edge of existence
The greatest conceivable being must exist, since if it didn't, it wouldn't be greatest.
The greatest conceivable being must exist.
The greatest conceivable being must exist.
  • For St. Anselm, God is part of the context of disclosure
  • God is that which grounds the particularity of the world
  • That the world is unthinkable without God is the context in which the world (and God) are seen
  • God is wholly other: he creates from nothing, and we reach him only by analogy from his creation

Conclusion

  • Anselm's “God” is grounded in the uniqueness of God with respect to creation
  • Consequence of creatio ex nihilo
  • God the context of disclosure in which everything is seen
