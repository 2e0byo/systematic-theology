Nicea I (325)

Canons: standing anyone?

We believe in One God, the Father Almighty, maker of all things visible and invisible. And in one Lord Jesus Christ the Son of God, the only-begotten born of the Father, who is of the substance of the Father, God from God, Light from Light, True God from True God, Begotten natum, not Made, consubstantial with the father, through whom all things were made in heaven and on earth, who for our salvation came down, was incarnate and made Man, suffered and rose the third day and ascended into heaven, he will come to judge the living and the dead. And [we believe] in the Holy Ghost.

But those who say "He was; and at one time he was not", and "before he was born, he was not" and "who was made from nothing" or from another substance or essence, or that the Son of God is changeable or mutable---these the Catholic Church anathematises.

— DZ125

Constantinople 1 (381)

- Creed (or did it?) - Ecumenical? - ‘Damnatio hæresium Subordinationorum et Modalistarum’ (DS 150)

Canon 1

The Creed of the three hundred and eighteen Fathers who were assembled at Nicaea in Bithynia is not to be set aside, but is to remain in force; 452 and every heresy is to be anathematized, and especially that of the Eunomians or Anomoians, and that of the Arians or Eudoxians, and that of the semi-Arians or Pneumatomachians, and that of the Sabellians, and that of the Marcellians, and that of the Photinians, and that of the Apollinarists.
Jurgens, Faith of the Early Fathers 910b
- Eunomian :: christ not God (unlike) - Eudoxian :: Homoian - Semi-Arians :: here re. Holy Ghost - Sabellians :: Modalists - Photians :: Modalists - Appolinarists :: monophysitism [Christ no human mind]

Canon 4

In regard to Maximus the Cynic and the disorders which took place in Constantinople on account of him, we declare that Maximus neither is nor ever was a bishop, and that a cleric of whatsoever rank ordained by him is is not ordained at all, since all things undertaken in his behalf and by him are invalid.
Jurgens, Faith of the Early Fathers 910e
Fn: The canon pronounces his consecration invalid, and therefore equally invalid, any ordinations he may have performed. First Constantinople is too early for us to expect any clear distinction between what is invalid and what is valid but illicit: yet, the disorders of this miserable affair were so great that the Fathers of Constantinople, in view of the extremely rigid laws of the early Church in respect to jurisdiction, very probably did quite right in proclaiming Maximus’ orders invalid, and not merely wrongly gotten. Certainly the Church can make laws pertinent not only to the licit but also to the valid administration of the Sacraments. The Church today, for example, requires jurisdiction for a valid administration of the Sacrament of Penance. It is not too strong, I think, to say that the Church in the fourth century required jurisdiction for the valid (maximus ordained night egyptian bishops + sailors in Gregory's church; kicked out and finished in house of flute-player!)

Chalcedon (451)

we all teach that with one accord we confess one and the same Son, our Lord Jesus Christ, the same perfect in human nature, truly God and the same with a rational soul and a body truly man, consubstantial with the Father according to divinity, and consubstantial with us according to human nature, like unto us in all things except sin; ... born of the Father before the ages according to divine nature, but in the last days ... born of the virgin Mary, Mother of God according to human nature; for us and for our deliverance, one and the same Christ only begotten Son, our Lord, acknowledged in two natures, without mingling, without change, indivisibly, undividedly, the distinction of the natures nowhere removed on account of the union but rather the peculiarity of each nature being kept, and uniting in one person and substance, not divided or separated into two persons, but one and the same Son ...
DS 148 (DZ301)

Constantinople II (553)

- anti arian
If anyone who agrees that a union has been born of the two natures of divinity and humanity, or who says that one nature of the Word of God has been made flesh, does not accept these (expressions) as the holy Fathers have taught, namely, that of the nature of God and of that of man, the union having taken place according to subsistence, one Christ was produced; but from such words attempts to introduce one nature or substance of Godhead and humanity of Christ, let such be anathema.
Canon 8, DS 220 (D Z429)

Constantinople III (681)

And so we proclaim two natural wills in Him, and two natural operations indivisibly, inconvertibly, inseparably, unfusedly according to the doctrine of the holy Father, and two natural wills not contrary, God forbid, according as impious heretics have asserted, but the human will following and not resisting or hesitating, but rather even submitting to His divine and omnipotent will.
DS 291 (DZ 289)

Nicea II (781)

We... define in all certitude and diligence that as the figure of the honoured and life-giving Cross, so the venerable and holy images ... must be suitably placed in the holy churches of God, both on sacred vessels and vestments, and on the walls and on the altars, at home and on the streets, namely such images of our Lord Jesus Christ, God and Savior, and of our undefiled lady, or holy Mother of God, and of the honorable angels, and, at the same time, of all the saints and of holy men. For, how much more frequently through the imaginal formation they are seen, so much more quickly are those who contemplate these, raised to the memory and desire of the originals of these, to kiss and to render honourable adoration to them, not however, to grant true Latria ... which is proper to divine nature alone; but just as to the figure of the revered and life-giving Cross and to the holy gospels let an oblation of incense and lights be made to give honour.... "For the honour of the image passes to the original"; and he who shows reverence to the image, shows reverence to the substance of Him depicted in it.
DS 302 (DZ 600)
